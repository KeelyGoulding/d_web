South China Tiger

Fast Facts

  • Population: <100 in captivity
  • Believed to be extinct in the wild
  • Region: South East China
  • Biggest Threats: Hunting, habitat loss

Description

This breed of tiger once roamed South Eastern China, but today it is beleived to be extinct in the wild, meaning the only ones left are being bred in captivity. Since the 1950s, the South China Tiger's population has decreased from around 4000 in the wild to as few as 50 in captivity. A South China Tiger can gorw up to 265 cm length and 175 kg, and they are the second-smallest subspecies of tiger. They have broader, widely spaced stripes, and females are smaller and lighter than males. These tigers are currently being reintroduced to the wild.

Threats

Habitat loss is the leading threat to the South China Tiger, as it has become severely fragmentes, with most fragments measuring less than 200sq miles. This means that their native habitat is too small to sustain a tiger population, and fragmentation leads to lack of breeding or cross-breeding. South China Tigers were also hunted as pests throughout history, although it was banned in 1979 it may already be too late. (Source)
